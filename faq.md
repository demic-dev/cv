### Tell us briefly about a past engineering project you’ve worked on. Please describe the problem you were trying to solve, what technical approach/solution you used and any impact you had.

The project I'm proudest of is from my first job, right out of high school, at a small software agency. I was the only dev and technical person, and my job was to turn client specs  into published apps. In the beginning, everything was manual and for every new app I'd have to set up the project by hand by adding client's configs, built it and release. It would take about an hour each time and it was easy to mess up.

So I built a tool to do the whole thing for me. You give it the client's specs, it clones a base repo from GitHub, injects the config, and builds the app with Expo for both stores. I like that I pulled this off with no senior devs around and none of the AI tools everyone has now, because back then automating the whole thing wasn't even an obvious move. It got deployment down from an hour to about 15 minutes, killed most of the manual mistakes, and meant one person could keep up with ~100 client apps. Because of all of this and because of my early seniority, it's one of the intuitions I am most proud as of today. The stack was TypeScript, React Native, GraphQL, Expo.


### A project you're proud about?

My most recent work experience is the thing I am most proud of in my career so far. My thesis research project consisted of analysing Reddit networks in order to evaluate a measure of ideological polarization. Polarization is a known measure that has already been tackled in the academic world, but this time it was tried with a different approach. Instead of undirected networks, which could possibly lose information by losing directionality, I used directed networks by using an operator borrowed from physics: the magnetic Laplacian. Beside the Reddit Network implementation which was relatively straightforward since it built on my advisor's prior work, what made me particularly proud was the complementary validation: since this was the first use of the operator in this context, in order to empirically validate the results, I ran simulations and experiments on randomly generated networks and null models to test whether the measure worked reliably across different contexts. After that, both the real-world and randomly generated networks were correlated with known network properties that are used in literature to evaluate polarization, and I found a 50% stronger correlation than the undirected variant. I am particularly proud of this because I managed to work close with my lab's researchers and because I was the first to apply this measure of polarization to directed networks, building on existing methods in a new way.
